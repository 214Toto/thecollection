BIOENERGETICS

Define Metabolism
✓ it is defined as the sum total of all the chemical reactions that are taking place in the body
✓ It includes the process by which cells use food material to obtain energy, store excess calories for future use and build up various substances.
Define Bioenergrtics
✓ is the fi eld of biochemistry that deals with transformation and utilization of energy in biological systems
What two important purposes does metabolism Serve ?
✓ To release energy from the ingested food material through catabolic degradation, and to convert this energy into a form that can be used for cellular work
✓ To transform small organic compounds into macromolecules
the energy inherent in the organic molecules is released, then later trapped and stored as.......?
✓ adenosine triphosphate (ATP)
List some of the major cellular works that ATP is used for ?
✓ Transport across cell membrane
✓ Muscle contraction
✓ Nerve conduction
✓ Transfer of genetic information
✓ Synthesis of biomolecules
Describe the interdependency and interconnection of metabolic reactions
✓ Metabolic reactions do not occur in isolation but rather in multi-step sequences called metabolic pathways
✓ Where the product of one reaction serves as a substrate for the next one; the product of second reaction is substrate for the third reaction, and so on
Define Energy & thermodynamics
✓ Energy is defined as capacity to do work; it is required for performing various activities
✓ thermodynamics is a branch of physical science that deals with energy changes.
What does the first Laws of thermodynamics state and elaborate some examples of it
✓ states that energy can neither be created nor destroyed. In course of any physical or chemical reaction, one form of energy may change to some other form, but the total amount of energy in the universe always remains constant
✓ For example, electric energy changes to heat energy in room heater
State the 2nd Law of Thermodynamics
✓ All physical and chemical reactions tend to proceed in such a direction that useful energy of the reacting system is irreversibly converted to a randomized and useless form, known as entropy
Useful energy is a type of energy that can be broadly classified into two types, namely ?
✓ Heat energy => which is capable of performing work through change of temperature
✓ Free Energy => which is capable of performing work at a constant temperature.
What does the term Enthalpy and entropy mean ?
✓ Enthalpy => change in heat content of the system
✓ Entropy => The randomized energy created from a chemical reaction
WHat is the relationship between Free energy, Enthalpy, entropy and temperature ?
in the relationship stated above, why is entropy usually presented to be Positive and ΔG presented to be Negative ?
✓ In course of any ongoing reaction, entropy always increases. Therefore, has a positive sign
✓ Since rise in entropy is accompanied by a corresponding fall in free energy of the system, the value of ΔG is negative
When is the Enthalpy taken to be + and when is it taken to be - ?
✓ It is positive when the heat absorption occurs and is negative when heat release occurs
WHat is the significance of entropy ?
✓ Since increase in entropy is an irreversible process, it gives direction to all biological activities.
WHy is Free energy considered to be the most important thermodynamic function ?
✓ because it is the driving force of the reactions
What does a negative and positive ΔG represent ?
✓ A negative sign of ΔG indicates an exergonic reaction
✓ A positive sign of ΔG indicates an endergonic reaction
Differentiate an exergonic reaction from a endergonic reaction
✓ exergonic reaction, the one that proceeds spontaneously and forms product from subratate. And free energy is released
✓ endergonic reaction is one which can proceed only in the backward direction. Free energy is required in this type of reaction
To obtain a convenient energetic expression that predicts the equilibrium of the reaction, we have to define the standard free energy change. what are the standard conditions to be considere during this procedure
✓ Temperature of 25°C (298°K).
✓ Pressure of 1.0 atm (760 mmHg).
✓ pH 7.0.
✓ The reactants and products are all present in the concentration of 1.0 M
Define The standard free energy change
✓ It is an immutable constant that equals the difference in energy levels between the substrate and products, under standard conditions
Differentiate Standard Free Energy change ΔG°' from actual Free Energy Change ΔG.
✓ ΔG°' is measured under standard condistions stated above while ΔG is measuredunder the conditions prevailing during the reaction
✓ ΔG in an ongoing reaction keeps changing with the progress of the reaction while ΔG°' is an immutable constant for a given reaction
What is the relationship between the two; ΔG°' and ΔG
how do you determine the Standard Free Energy Changes of Sequential Reactions
✓ Total standard free energy change of a reaction sequence equals the sum of the standard free energy changes of the individual reactions of the sequence
Describe the structure of ATP
✓ Has a purine Base, Adenine
✓ Ribose sugar, Linked to N-9 of the adenine through N-glycosidic linkage at its 1st carbon
✓ Phosphate groups, three in number, are designated α-, β- and γ-phosphates. The α-phosphate is linked to C-5 of the ribose.
State some of the functions of ATP
✓ ATP serves as a link between the energy-yielding (exergonic) pathways and the energy-requiring (endergonic) processes.
✓ Used as a storage form of free energy
WHy is ATP referred to as the currency of free energy in the body
✓ because ATP serves as a primary and universal carrier of free energy
The standard free energy of hydrolysis of ATP is -7.3 kcal/mole. What does that means
✓ It implies that 7.3 kcal of free energy is released, under standard conditions, from 1.0 mole of ATP
Explain the concept of ATP Cycle
✓ Hydrolysis of ATP occurs, which liberates the required energy
✓ generation of ATP is followed by its hydrolysis, which is again followed by regeneration, and so on.
✓ Thus the γ-phosphate group of ATP continually undergoes removal and replacement in a cyclic fashion
What are Low energy compounds and give an exmaple.
✓ These are compounds having a ΔG°' less than that of ATP.
✓ Examples include; Glucose 6-phosphate, Glucose 1-phosphate and Glycerol 1-phosphate
What are High energy compounds and give an exmaple.
✓ These are compounds that have the same ΔG°' as that of ATP
What are Super High energy compounds and give an exmaple.
✓ thses are compounds with a ΔG°' higher than that of ATP
✓ Phosphoenol pyruvate, Carbamoyl phosphate, etc
While giving Phosphoenol pyruvate as an exmaple, explain the concept of substrate level phosphorylation
✓ Looking at a Super High ENergy compound like PEP, its conversion to pyruvate in the glycolitic pathway yeilds 14.8 kcal/mole energy
✓ This being more than enough fo rthe formation of ATP leads to AT formation coupled with transformation of a substrate (i.e. PEP in this case)
Where is creatine Phosphate mostly found ?
✓ stored in the skeletal muscles
What is orthophosphate cleavage and what is its result ?
✓ Removal o fthe terminal γ-phosphate group from ATP to form ADP
✓ it yeilds -7.3kcal/mol of ΔG°'
Describe the pyrophosphate cleavage and its significance
✓ This is when the β-and the γ-phosphate groups are simultaneously removed in one piece to form AMP and pyrophosphate
✓ This proceeds releases -14.6 kcal/mole of ΔG°', which is sufficient to drive certain reactions like fatty acid activation, with the excess energy dissipating as entropy
How does ADP returns into the ATP cycle ?
✓ by directly accepting a phosphate group to become ATP
How does AMP returns into the ATP cycle ?
✓ It requires adenylate kinase, which catalyzes reversible phosphorylation of AMP to yield two ADPs
✓ The ADPs so formed directly accept phosphate group(s) to form ATP
What other role does adenylate kinase have ?
✓ it helps to maintain a constant intracellular ATP level by catalyzing the transfer of the β-phosphate of one ADP to another ADP molecule
✓ Through this same process, it also supplimnets the ATP generation by creatine kinase in the contracting muscles
Aside ATP, all cells have several energy-rich compounds that are analogous structures of ATP, Namely ?
✓ guanosine triphosphate (GTP)
✓ cytidine triphosphate (CTP)
✓ uridine triphosphate (UTP)
Inpspite of being in lower concentration than ATP these Compounds are equally as useful as ATP, WHY ?
✓ nucleoside triphosphates are specialized to serve in certain biosynthetic pathways.
✓ or example, CTP is used in phospholipid biosynthesis and UTP in glycogen synthesis.
What components are required in the synthesis of the nucleoside triphosphates ?
✓ enzymes called nucleoside diphosphokinase
✓ ATP mostly serves as the donor of the terminal phosphate groups
Low concentration of corresponding 2’-deoxyribonucleoside 5’-triphosphates is also present in cells. List Them.
✓ 2’-deoxyadenosine 5’-triphosphate (dATP)
✓ 2’-deoxyguanosine 5’-triphosphate (dGTP)
✓ 2’-deoxycytidine 5’-triphosphate (dCTP)
✓ 2’-deoxythymidine 5’- triphosphate (dTTP)
